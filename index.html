Abstract

As per World Health Organizationâ€™s report, 1.3 billion people on earth live with some kind of visual impairment out of which 5.1% are caused by some sort of corneal diseases making it the 4th cause of blindness globally. About 80% of these diseases are avoidable. Specular endothelial microscopy imaging (SEMI) is a non-invasive technique that provides information about the physiological reserve from aging and general health of the corneal endothelial. Cell morphology parameters found from SEMI are used to check corneal health after surgical procedures, pharmaceutical exposures, and trauma. They also play a prominent role in the selection donated corneal samples to identify suitable samples for transplant surgeries. The methods currently in use are resource and effort consuming. This results in their limited use in global low-resource settings, and is also a significant obstacle in the establishment of eye-banks, a prerequisite for performing corneal transplants.

The research group at Michigan Technological University along with the research group from John Hopkins Medical School, is developing a new imaging and image analysis technique to reduce the overall time, cost and resources needed to utilize SEMI globally.

Introduction

Originally designed by Maurice to provide a high magnification view of the specular reflected light from the corneal endothelium, it has since undergone modifications and currently serves as the preferred method for corneal endothelial cell density (ECD) determination. It is the traditional measure of corneal health in a wide variety of corneal procedures and corneal endotheliopathies. In penetrating keratoplasty, ECD is one of the factors used to predict outcomes for the procedure. It is also routinely used in clinical practice to monitor the status of the corneal endothelium in other ocular surgical procedures, transplants, pharmaceutical exposure, genetic disorders, safety of new drugs, and normal aging. With the development of new refractive procedures, such as phakic intraocular lenses intraocular telescopes, new topical and intraocular drugs and solutions, there is a growing need for standardization and validation of corneal endothelial specular microscopy.

Ancillary testing and evaluation of images is typically conducted by support staff through either the (1) comparison method, (2) frame method (fixed or variable), (3) corner method, and (4) flex-center method. Currently, non-contract specular microscopes such as the Konan CellChek SL are routinely used to provide reliable images for ECD determination. Images are then analyzed using CellChek software. Which provides automated analysis and semi-automated analysis methods. However, automated analysis methods have been shown to significantly overestimated ECD in eyes with high polymegathism and/or large cell size. Furthermore, cost associated with the use of commercially available non-contrast specular microscopes prevent its global use in low-resource settings and provide.

We propose a new method for the procurement of corneal images through the use of slit-lamp in conjunction with a smartphone capable of obtaining images at 4K and 60fps settings. We also propose the automated analysis of these images by utilizing deep learning and image-recognition frameworks.

Methods & Strategies

Smartphone based corneal images are obtained with the patient is seated at a slit-lamp biomicroscope and the head secured in the headrest. The microscope is dialed to 40X magnification and a beam of 0.2mm diameter directed toward the cornea 60 degrees away from the viewer to obtain specular reflection, a reflection of light from the corneal endothelium. The smartphone is set to maximum zoom and placed directly anterior to the optic such that the light reflecting from the endothelium is centered in the camera. The video recording is started with a minimum of 4K and 60fps settings. After two minutes, the frame with the highest resolution of cell borders is selected for analysis.

The project is divided into two stages. The first stage is focused on the analysis of available datasets using classical image processing and computer vision techniques. The goal is to compute differences between the images taken from smartphone devices and the ones taken using the existing medical devices such as Konan. Both PI and Co-PI research groups are also concurrently involved in data collection and image-labelling. The second step involves the use of deep neural network (DNN) to train an architecture to detect cellular morphology and diseases from the images taken from the mobile phone. The ultimate goal of this project is to create a well-trained DNN-model that could be efficient enough to work on a smartphone device hardware and robust enough to work under different circumstances and illuminations with highest accuracy.
